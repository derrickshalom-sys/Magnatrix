How It Works

The MagnaCartridge Process

Three integrated stages from raw waste to fully recycled material

01
🧼

Pretreatment Stage

Incoming plastic feedstock undergoes a rigorous preparation phase in which it is thoroughly cleaned to remove surface contaminants, then thermally conditioned to an optimal temperature that maximises subsequent enzymatic activity. This stage ensures the material enters the core processing chamber in a standardised, chemically receptive state.

02
🧲

MagnaCartridge Stage

The conditioned plastic is introduced into the MagnaCartridge chamber, where an array of precision-engineered magnetic balls and baffles directs flow and maximises surface contact. External magnets and embedded sensors regulate the movement of these elements in real time. Enzyme solutions residing within the chamber act upon the polymer chains as the plastic passes through, systematically cleaving molecular bonds and degrading the material into its constituent monomers.

03
♻️

Output & Reassembly Stage

The resulting monomer stream is collected and repolymerised into virgin-quality plastic material, completing the closed-loop recycling cycle. In future refined iterations of the MagnaCartridge, an integrated output selection interface will allow operators to specify the desired end product for instance, directing the reassembly process toward a particular form factor such as a water bottle, enabling on-demand, application-specific material manufacturing.

About Us

We're rethinking plastic
From the Ground Up

Magnatrix was founded on a simple belief: the plastic crisis isn't a material problem it's an engineering problem. Existing recycling infrastructure is inefficient, costly, and ultimately unable to scale to the volume of plastic waste the world produces every day.

Our team of biochemical engineers and materials scientists is developing the MagnaCartridge a compact, modular machine that uses enzyme-based chemistry to depolymerize plastic waste into reusable raw materials, on-site and at low cost.

We believe the solution to plastic pollution lies not in managing waste better, but in eliminating the concept of waste entirely.
